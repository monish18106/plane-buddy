The Rocket Plane That Reached Space

The North American X-15 was a pioneering rocket-powered aircraft developed in the 1950s to explore the edge of space. Operated by NASA and the U.S. Air Force, it reached altitudes over 100 km and speeds exceeding Mach 6.7, setting records that still stand today. Its data helped shape future spacecraft design, making it one of the most important experimental aircraft in history.

Notable Features

  • Equipped with a powerful XLR99 rocket engine using liquid oxygen and ammonia fuel.
  • Achieved record-breaking speeds and altitudes that qualified some pilots as astronauts.
  • Provided critical data for spaceflight re-entry, thermal protection, and control at hypersonic speeds.
  • Launched from a Boeing B-52 “mothership” at high altitude before igniting its rocket engine.
  • Played a vital role in advancing technology for the Apollo and Space Shuttle programs.

Legacy and Impact

The North American X-15 remains one of the most successful experimental aircraft programs in history. Its flights bridged the gap between atmospheric flight and space exploration, providing data used in the development of spacecraft like the Apollo Command Module and later the Space Shuttle.

Thirteen of its pilots earned astronaut wings for flights exceeding 50 miles in altitude. The X-15’s pioneering achievements in speed, altitude, and aerospace research paved the way for modern hypersonic vehicles and remain a testament to human innovation and courage.
